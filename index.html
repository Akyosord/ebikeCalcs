functions How It Works — Full Derivation

1. The Motor — Converting Electricity to Spinning

A brushless DC (BLDC) hub motor has permanent magnets on the wheel and copper coils on the axle. When current flows through the coils, electromagnetic force pushes against the magnets and spins the wheel.

Two constants define any motor:

  • Torque constant \( K_t \) — torque produced per ampere of current:
$$ T = K_t \cdot I $$
  • Back-EMF constant \( K_e \) — voltage generated per unit of angular velocity:
$$ E = K_e \cdot \omega $$

The key identity for BLDC motors (from energy conservation):

$$ K_t = K_e \quad \text{(in SI units: Nm/A} = \text{V}{\cdot}\text{s/rad)} $$

A motor that produces lots of torque per amp also generates lots of back-EMF per unit speed. You can't have one without the other.

2. The Electrical Equation — Voltage Budget

The battery voltage is a fixed "budget" that must be divided among three consumers:

$$ V_{oc} = \underbrace{K_e \cdot \omega}_{\text{back-EMF}} + \underbrace{I \cdot R_{\text{wind}}}_{\text{motor heat}} + \underbrace{I_{\text{bat}} \cdot R_{\text{bat}}}_{\text{battery sag}} $$

As speed \(\omega\) increases, the back-EMF term \(K_e \omega\) grows, leaving less voltage to push current. Eventually, at top speed, nearly all the voltage is consumed by back-EMF and only a trickle of current flows.

3. Deriving \( K_t \) from Top Speed

At top speed the bike is in steady state — no acceleration. The motor only needs to overcome drag and rolling resistance:

$$ F_{\text{drag}} = \frac{1}{2} \rho \cdot C_d A \cdot v_{\text{top}}^2 $$ $$ F_{\text{roll}} = C_r \cdot m \cdot g $$ $$ P_{\text{cruise}} = (F_{\text{drag}} + F_{\text{roll}}) \cdot v_{\text{top}} $$

The cruise current drawn from the battery:

$$ I_{\text{cruise}} \approx \frac{P_{\text{cruise}}}{V_{oc}} $$

For a hub motor (gear ratio \(G=1\)), the motor's angular velocity at top speed:

$$ \omega_{\text{top}} = \frac{v_{\text{top}}}{R_{\text{wheel}}} $$

Substituting into the voltage equation at top speed and solving for \(K_e\):

$$ \boxed{K_t = K_e = \frac{V_{oc} - I_{\text{cruise}} \cdot (R_{\text{wind}} + R_{\text{bat}})}{\omega_{\text{top}}}} $$

Why does this work? At top speed, we know everything on the right side: the battery voltage \(V_{oc}\) is on the label, \(I_{\text{cruise}}\) is small (just overcoming drag), the resistances are estimated, and \(\omega_{\text{top}} = v_{\text{top}} / R_w\). The only unknown is \(K_e\), so we solve for it. It's just rearranging the voltage equation at the one operating point we can fully characterize.

4. Three Constraint Zones & Transition Points

At any speed, three independent limits each cap the motor current. The lowest one wins:

Zone 1: Phase Current Limit (constant torque)

$$ I_1 = I_{\text{phase,max}} \quad \Rightarrow \quad T_1 = K_t \cdot I_{\text{phase,max}} $$

At low speed, back-EMF is small. The controller's PWM duty cycle is low, so it can boost phase current well above battery current (buck converter effect). The controller's hardware phase-current limit is the binding constraint. Torque is constant — this is your hardest launch.

Zone 2: Battery Current Limit (constant power)

The battery delivers a fixed max power:

$$ P_{\text{bat}} = (V_{oc} - I_{\text{bat,max}} \cdot R_{\text{bat}}) \cdot I_{\text{bat,max}} $$

Here \(E\) is shorthand for the back-EMF at the current speed: \(E = K_e \cdot \omega\). The motor's voltage equation is:

$$ V_{\text{motor}} = E + I \cdot R_{\text{wind}} $$

Power delivered to the motor: \(P = V_{\text{motor}} \cdot I = (E + I \cdot R_{\text{wind}}) \cdot I\). Expanding:

$$ R_{\text{wind}} \cdot I^2 + E \cdot I - P_{\text{bat}} = 0 $$

This is a quadratic in \(I\). Applying the quadratic formula (\(ax^2+bx+c=0\)) and taking the positive root:

$$ I_2 = \frac{-E + \sqrt{E^2 + 4 R_{\text{wind}} \cdot P_{\text{bat}}}}{2 R_{\text{wind}}} $$

As speed rises, \(E\) grows, so \(I_2\) decreases — the motor can accept less current. Physically: maintaining phase current requires more power (motor voltage = \(E + IR\) rises). When the battery can't provide it, the controller reduces phase current. Torque falls roughly as \(1/\omega\) — constant power region.

Zone 3: Voltage Limit (back-EMF dominated)

$$ I_3 = \frac{V_{oc} - K_e \cdot \omega}{R_{\text{wind}} + R_{\text{bat}}} $$

At very high speed, the back-EMF \(K_e \omega\) approaches the battery voltage even at 100% duty cycle. Current (and torque) drops linearly to zero. This is the "wall" approaching top speed.

When Do the Transitions Happen?

The bike switches from Zone 1 → Zone 2 when the battery power needed to sustain full phase current equals the battery's max output:

$$ \text{Zone 1→2 transition speed:} \quad v_{12} \approx \frac{P_{\text{bat,max}}}{K_t \cdot I_{\text{phase,max}} / R_w} $$ $$ \text{i.e., when } \quad K_e \cdot \omega + I_{\text{phase,max}} \cdot R_{\text{wind}} \approx \frac{P_{\text{bat}}}{I_{\text{phase,max}}} $$

It switches from Zone 2 → Zone 3 when the voltage-limited current drops below the battery-limited current:

$$ \text{Zone 2→3 transition:} \quad \frac{V_{oc} - K_e \omega}{R_{\text{wind}} + R_{\text{bat}}} < I_2(\omega) $$

Physical intuition:

  • Zone 1→2: "The battery can't keep up." You're demanding more power than it can supply at that current limit.
  • Zone 2→3: "The motor is fighting itself." Back-EMF is so large that even with the full battery voltage applied, current is physically limited by Ohm's law.

5. From Torque to Acceleration

The actual motor current at any speed is the minimum of all three constraints:

$$ I_{\text{actual}}(\omega) = \min(I_1,\; I_2,\; I_3) $$

Motor torque converts to force at the wheel (hub motor):

$$ F_{\text{traction}} = \frac{K_t \cdot I_{\text{actual}}}{R_{\text{wheel}}} $$

Net force after subtracting drag and rolling resistance:

$$ F_{\text{net}} = F_{\text{traction}} - \frac{1}{2}\rho \, C_dA \, v^2 - C_r \, m \, g $$

Newton's second law gives instantaneous acceleration:

$$ \boxed{a = \frac{F_{\text{net}}}{m}} $$

The simulation integrates this using Euler's method with \(\Delta t = 0.05\,\text{s}\):

$$ v(t + \Delta t) = v(t) + a(t) \cdot \Delta t $$

6. Why Higher Wattage ≠ Faster Launch

For a hub motor, combining the Kt derivation with the launch force formula:

$$ F_{\text{launch}} = \frac{K_t \cdot I_{\text{phase}}}{R_w} = \frac{V_{oc}}{v_{\text{top}}} \cdot I_{\text{phase}} \cdot \frac{R_w}{R_w} $$ $$ \boxed{F_{\text{launch}} \approx \frac{V_{oc} \cdot I_{\text{phase}}}{v_{\text{top}}}} $$

The wheel radius cancels! Launch force depends only on voltage, phase current, and top speed. A "9000W" motor with higher top speed has lower Kt, producing less launch force at the same current than a "6000W" motor with lower top speed.

\(\text{Power} = \text{Torque} \times \text{Speed}\). High wattage can come from modest torque at high RPM.

7. DC vs BLDC — Why This Model Works

All the equations above look like a brushed DC motor. That's intentional — a 3-phase BLDC motor with trapezoidal commutation (which all e-bike controllers use) is electrically equivalent to a brushed DC motor.

The controller's job is to electronically switch (commutate) the phases so that current always flows through the correct coils — exactly what brushes do mechanically. The result:

ConceptBrushed DCBLDC (trapezoidal)
\(V = E + IR\)YesYes (per-phase, controller handles switching)
\(K_t = K_e\)YesYes (using line-to-line constants)
Torque ∝ currentYesYes
Back-EMF ∝ speedYesYes

What would break the model:

  • Field-Oriented Control (FOC) with field weakening — extends the constant-power zone past the nominal voltage limit by phase-shifting current. Budget e-bike controllers don't do this.
  • Interior permanent magnet (IPM) motors — have reluctance torque that doesn't follow \(T = K_t I\). E-bike hub motors are surface-magnet (SPM), so this doesn't apply.

Bottom line: For surface-magnet BLDC with trapezoidal control (= all e-bike hub motors), the DC motor equations are exact. The "B" in BLDC just means commutation is done by transistors instead of carbon brushes.

8. The Voltage Slider — State of Charge

A lithium battery's voltage varies with state of charge (SOC):

$$ V_{\text{actual}} = V_{\text{nominal}} \times (1 + \text{slider}\%) $$
Fully charged (100% SOC):\(\approx +15\%\) above nominal
Mid charge (50% SOC):\(0\%\) — nominal
Nearly empty (20% SOC):\(\approx -14\%\) below nominal
Under heavy load:Additional sag: \(\Delta V = I_{\text{bat}} \cdot R_{\text{bat}}\)

Higher voltage → more headroom above back-EMF → more current → more torque. This is why the bike feels noticeably punchier on a full charge.
