functions How It Works — Full Derivation

1. The Motor — Converting Electricity to Spinning

A brushless DC (BLDC) hub motor has magnets on the wheel and copper coils on the axle. When current flows through the coils, it pushes against the magnets and makes the wheel spin.

Two numbers define any motor:

  • Kt (torque constant) — how much torque you get per amp: T = K_t × I
  • Ke (back-EMF constant) — how much voltage the motor generates when spinning: E = K_e × ω

The magic: for any BLDC motor, Kt = Ke (in SI units). They're the same number! A motor that gives lots of torque per amp also generates lots of voltage per speed.

2. The Electrical Equation — Voltage Budget

Think of the battery voltage as a "budget" that gets spent on three things:

Vbat = E + I × Rwinding + Ibat × Rbattery

Where:

  • E = Ke × ω — back-EMF (voltage "used up" by the motor spinning)
  • I × Rwinding — voltage lost as heat in the motor coils
  • Ibat × Rbattery — voltage lost inside the battery (sag)

As you go faster, the back-EMF E grows. Eventually it uses up almost all the battery voltage, and there's nothing left to push current through → no more torque → you stop accelerating. That's your top speed.

3. Finding Kt from Top Speed

At top speed, you're no longer accelerating — the motor just needs enough torque to fight air drag and tire friction. We use this to work backwards:

Step 1: Drag force at top speed
Fdrag = ½ × ρ × CdA × vtop²
Step 2: Rolling resistance
Froll = Cr × m × g
Step 3: Power needed to cruise at top speed
Pcruise = (Fdrag + Froll) × vtop
Step 4: Current at cruise
Icruise = Pcruise / Voc
Step 5: Motor speed (hub motor, G=1)
ω = vtop / Rwheel
Step 6: Solve for Ke
Ke = (Voc − Icruise × (Rwind + Rbat)) / ω

Since Kt = Ke, we now know the motor's torque constant from just the top speed and battery voltage.

4. Three Speed Zones — What Limits You When

At any speed, the motor current is limited by whichever constraint is tightest:

Zone 1: Phase Current Limit (low speed)

I = Iphase_max (set by controller)

At low speed, back-EMF is small, so the controller can push maximum current into the motor. Torque is constant: T = Kt × Iphase_max

This is your launch acceleration.

Zone 2: Battery Current Limit (mid speed)

Pavailable = Vbat × Ibat_max
As speed ↑, power demand rises → battery current hits its limit
Torque must drop: T = P / (ω × Rwheel)

The controller can no longer maintain full phase current because the battery can't supply enough power. You're in "constant power" mode — torque falls as 1/speed.

Zone 3: Voltage Limit (high speed)

I = (Vbat − Ke × ω) / (Rwind + Rbat)

Back-EMF is so high it nearly equals battery voltage. Very little current can flow regardless of limits. Torque drops linearly to zero at the no-load speed.

5. From Torque to Acceleration

Once we know torque at any speed, Newton's 2nd law gives acceleration:

Force at wheel (hub motor):
F = T / Rwheel = Kt × I / Rwheel
Subtract drag and rolling resistance:
Fnet = F − Fdrag − Froll
Newton's 2nd law:
a = Fnet / m

The simulation computes this at tiny time steps (0.05s), updating speed each step. That traces out the full speed-vs-time curve you see in the chart.

6. Why Higher Wattage ≠ Faster Launch

For hub motors on the same voltage:

Launch force = Voc × Iphase / vtop

If a "9000W" motor achieves its rating by having higher top speed (lower Kt), then at the same phase current it actually has less launch torque than a "6000W" motor with lower top speed.

Wattage = torque × speed. You can have high watts from lots of torque at low speed, OR moderate torque at high speed. The spec sheet tells you total watts, not how they're split.

7. The Voltage Slider — State of Charge

A "72V" battery isn't always 72V:

  • Fully charged (100% SOC): ~84V (+15%) for a 20S lithium pack
  • Nominal (50% SOC): ~72V (0%)
  • Nearly empty (20% SOC): ~62V (−14%)
  • Under heavy load: further sag from internal resistance

Higher voltage → more current can flow at any speed → more torque → faster acceleration. This is why your bike feels faster on a full charge.
